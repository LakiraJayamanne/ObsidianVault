SLIDE 1 — The Enron Scandal (title slide)
----------------------------------------------
Hi, my name is Lakira Jayamanne and this presentation is about the Enron scandal — one of the biggest cases of corporate fraud in history.

In 2001, Enron collapsed, taking thousands of jobs with it and filing for the largest bankruptcy the US had ever seen.

I'm going to cover three key ways they pulled it off, and for each one, an IT solution that could have stopped it.


SLIDE 2 — From Hero to Zero in 11 Days
----------------------------------------------
In the year 2000, Enron was America's most celebrated company — a Fortune 500 giant worth over 60 billion dollars.

By December 2001, it had filed for bankruptcy. 5,600 employees lost their jobs practically overnight.

The question is: how did no one catch it? And what IT systems could have prevented it?


SLIDE 3 — Fraud Mechanism 1: Mark-to-Market Accounting
----------------------------------------------
The first method was mark-to-market accounting abuse.

Normally this is a legitimate technique — but Enron weaponised it. Say they signed a 10-year energy contract worth 100 million dollars. They'd book that entire amount as profit immediately, on day one, even if not a single dollar had come in yet.

This made their finances look incredible on paper, while in reality they were haemorrhaging cash.


SLIDE 4 — Profits on Paper, Nothing in the Bank
----------------------------------------------
You can see it clearly in the numbers. Reported profits soared year on year — but actual cash coming in was a fraction of what was claimed.

The gap between Enron's books and reality was wider than the entire legitimate business.

The IT solution here is a real-time cash flow monitoring system. It automatically compares reported profits against actual cash arriving. If the numbers don't match, it raises an alert — and executives can't manually override it.


SLIDE 5 — Fraud Mechanism 2: Special Purpose Entities
----------------------------------------------
The second method was hiding debt through shell companies.

CFO Andrew Fastow created hundreds of fake external companies — with names like LJM and Raptor — and transferred Enron's bad debts into them. On paper, the debt was gone.

But these companies were secretly backed by Enron's own stock and run by Fastow himself. The debt never actually left — it was just hidden from investors and regulators.


SLIDE 6 — How the SPE Network Operated
----------------------------------------------
The structure was essentially circular. Enron transferred assets to the shell companies, the shell companies borrowed against those assets, and then Enron secretly guaranteed that debt with its own stock.

So the losses never truly left Enron — they just cycled back in disguise.

The IT solution is an automated entity verification system. Any company Enron does business with gets instantly cross-referenced against registration databases and ownership records. If a supposedly external company is secretly linked back to Enron executives, the system flags it and the transaction can't proceed.


SLIDE 7 — Fraud Mechanism 3: Arthur Andersen
----------------------------------------------
The third and probably most disturbing factor was the failure of Arthur Andersen — Enron's auditing firm.

Andersen were being paid a million dollars a week by Enron — for both auditing and consulting. They knew something was wrong, but kept signing off on the accounts anyway.

When investigators came knocking, Andersen employees shredded tonnes of documents and deleted thousands of emails. The firm was convicted of obstruction of justice and collapsed entirely, ending a 90-year legacy.


SLIDE 8 — The Three Audit Failures
----------------------------------------------
So to summarise the three failures that allowed all of this to happen:

First — cash discrepancies were ignored. Reported profits bore no relation to actual cash, and nobody challenged it.

Second — entity verification never happened. The shell company ownership links back to Enron were never independently checked.

Third — there were no immutable records. Documents could be destroyed, and they were.


SLIDE 9 — What Modern IT Audit Would Have Caught
----------------------------------------------
Each of these failures maps directly to an IT solution that exists today.

Real-time cash flow monitoring would have made mark-to-market fraud immediately visible.

Third-party entity verification would have exposed the shell company network the moment it was created.

And an immutable audit trail — records stored on a write-once, independently managed system — would have made document destruction technically impossible. Shredding paper becomes irrelevant when everything is already backed up externally.


SLIDE 10 — Key Takeaways
----------------------------------------------
Enron's fraud lasted as long as it did because there was nothing in place to automatically catch it.

The three IT solutions I've outlined wouldn't have needed advanced technology. They would just have made it impossible to hide the truth.

Revenue without cash is a red flag. Related-party transactions must be transparent. And audit trails must be immutable.

Thanks for watching.
